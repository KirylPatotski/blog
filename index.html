Why I never considered going into medicine

Every parent that has ever stepped foot on earth would have been proud if their children became a doctor or a medical engineer. When introducing oneself, it guarantees respect - rightly so. Medicine is not easy. Doctors sacrifice a lot for their profession and that deserves respect.

Even my mother wanted me to be a doctor or a medical engineer. And that for a reason, I'm highly capable. Yet, medicine never interested me. Why so? That’s what I will explore here. I will share my arguments that reveal why careers in medicine are not a good idea in my opinion.

Who is this text for? To those of you who are not unshakably sure they want to go into medicine. If you are certain, then good luck with your path, and if you have been in medicine for decades, congratulations, and I hope your life is free of regret. But for those of you who are in a foggy situation, this text should be the lighthouse. This text is for those who have still a bit of doubt and are considering another option.

Why should you listen to me? I want to be a businessman. Above all, that’s stressful and will require me to give all of my energy daily. In that sense, being a businessman and a doctor is similar. Both require lifelong studying and true mastery lies within being a student. Sometimes everything gets reinvented. In medicine and high-end business, there will be moments when difficult decisions have to be made. Hence, business and medicine are similar. So, why should you listen to me? I thought everything through regarding my life and it proves itself correct. I live a decently exhausting version of life right now and observe myself. I also gained plentiful experience in making life-changing decisions, I planned my life a long time ago. Furthermore, I'm an experienced writer and thus have extraordinary reflective and rhetoric capabilities.

Let’s make a tactical decision

The rewards are the same everywhere. It’s easy to get distracted with all of the status you’ll gain as a doctor. You’ll be easily in the top percentile of society. Doesn’t it feel great to feel recognized and doesn’t it feel great to help people while being paid well? But let us not forget that status and salary are relatively irrelevant. Helping people is also irrelevant. Why is helping people irrelevant? Because every job contributes to society. A teacher is helping society just like a doctor. Furthermore, recognition, status, a high salary, and the feeling that you help people and that you are useful can be obtained in any realm of human endeavor. For example, being a businessman brings you status, money, and a deep sense of purpose, i.e. feeling useful. Being a teacher brings those rewards as well. Even being a housewife can bring extraordinary fulfillment. You get used to the rewards of your respective career very fast and they don’t differ too much across careers, hence they are irrelevant.

What's at the center? Now, if all rewards are the same everywhere, what should you look at? At the center. Look at what you will be actually doing every day.* You must think of the task you’re supposed to enter a flow with. Look at your daily tasks and decide whether they excite you

“As a doctor, I want to get in a flow while smelling blood and while chopping corpses. I want to study for ten years to neglect my future family just to get an even more exhausting job. I enjoy the hospital's piercing smell and its depressive interior design.” – text #119

Are you the right person? Frankly, I don’t know what kind of person wants to see suffering and cut flesh as their daily task in the first place. I can’t comprehend how someone can feel attracted to the pain of others. Not too long ago, I sat on a train and some medicine students were having a conversation. One of them was hot even, though she was a brunette. They were not talking about business, love, or even parties, instead, about what types of wounds there are. Are you attracted to that? I couldn’t do that and most others couldn’t.

Is the environment right for you? My parents, school, and everyone else wanted me to go into science. That’s the direction my life was heading. At 5 years old I was passionate about the universe and at 7 about nuclear physics. It looked as if I was going to be a scientist. At the age of 13, I started to desire being a businessman and then at the ages of 14 and 15, I was a bit split between being a scientist and being an entrepreneur. But then, guess what happened? I had an internship at my local university and I hated it. I’ve been misled my entire childhood. There were multiple reasons to hate it, for example, the atmosphere, the design of the buildings, the tasks but also the people. Surprisingly, I had nothing in common with those other scientists and students. I could not see myself growing up to be such a beta. This is not personal, but I don’t like engineers, electricians and stem professors as people. The way I think and act is incompatible with that of a physics geek. I once wrote a 5,000-word essay on this topic, which is unpublished, so describing the differences further would break this text. But generally, do you get along with the people there and the atmosphere? Personally, I would feel better in a school than in a sterile hospital that has this piercing smell on a night shift.

Live by the deepest realization. You may be tempted to think that being a doctor is more than just worrying about wounds. Sure, being a doctor is also about filling out documents, but again, that’s every job so that’s irrelevant. If blood scares you a bit but you view it as the price for entry, you are lying to yourself. Seeing blood is the entire job, not a price to entry. Do not attach your ego to decisions and make them living by the deepest realization.

What are you doing right now? Why am I unshakably certain I will be a businessman? Because that’s practically all I do. For years now, I have started and finished projects. I started to develop the skills I need or will need. Analogous to this, if you obsess over medicine already, you should pursue it. If you are not obsessed with medicine already, chances are, you never will. You cannot choose what to develop interest for just as you cannot decide who to fall in love with. Do you want to do something that will not interest you or be together with someone who you don’t love? Exactly. So, you can’t know whether something will interest you at all. Sure, you can fall in love just as you can develop passion, and it is likely, but there’s no guarantee. If you don’t occupy yourself with medicine all day long already, it is not your purpose and chances stand against you.

Consider this. Finally, your self-awareness may be zero. This is to warn you. You can find out whether you have self-awareness or not if (1) things and feedback offend you, (2) you avoid responsibility and think that people are not radically responsible for their lives, (3) you don’t do sports (I mean real sports), avoid the hardship of any kind in general, (4) you are addicted to something, for example, you use social media just consuming, (5) you are negative, (6) you are a thinker or dreamer, i.e. like to be right and live in your fantasy and finally (7) you don’t realize that when you are tired or hungry your thoughts and emotions start to change. If you have zero self-awareness, all of your decisions tend to be harmful. All of that is because (8) you refuse to meditate, reflect, and to journal. If you cannot make clear decisions, you have a problem and no one can help you because self-awareness is rare. There may not be enough time left to summon self-awareness, but to sincerely reflect, you’ve always got time.

Finally, think long term. If you start to reflect, make the subject of your mediation your changing priorities. If you think that you can study with diligence today, there’s no guarantee you’ll be able to study for the next decade. There’s no guarantee you will not lose interest in medicine. Also, what makes you think that your priorities will stay the same? Now with 18, you may think that helping people in medicine is noble. With 35, that could be irrelevant and you’ll have to handle other priorities, especially as a woman. Now you may desire a bit of status, but I can guarantee this will change once you grow up. Further. Now you are likely to think that fulfillment comes from the job. Will you think the same with 50 or will family bring fulfillment with 50? Yet, with 50 it may be too late to change things. Especially with zero self-awareness, it’s impossible to predict how you will change. However, change is guaranteed. You can’t know what life is going to give you. That’s why it’s interesting.

Be willing to pay the price. And… let’s not forget being a doctor is hard. You can forget about family. Studying for a decade is a huge sacrifice. You have to be willing to pay the price. For a bit of status, that must surely be worth it, right?

So, should you become a doctor? Well no, unless you want to give your life to this profession. If you are not unshakably sure, don’t do it. If you have zero self-awareness, be cautious. If you don’t enjoy flesh, blood, and pain, forget it. If you don’t occupy yourself with medicine all day long already, there’s no guarantee you will get interested in it. Don’t look at what your parents will think too much. Don’t look at the other rewards, because they are universal. If you don’t want to give your life to something difficult, there’s no point in trying to pursue it. Also, consider your changing priorities. Something difficult is only doable with purpose, with burning desire. Only with a strong enough why will you be able to stand almost any how. However, if you are uncertain, that ‘why’ doesn’t exist. Be willing to pay the price. Live by the deepest realization.

*By the way, an internship has nothing to do with the real job. If you did the real job, you would be paid. You saw two weeks and not four decades.
